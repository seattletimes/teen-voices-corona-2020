An unknown future
Riplee Norris, 14, is an 8th grade student at Glover Middle School in Spokane.

Every night my family watches the news. Before, when we watched it, I saw the weather, celebrity drama, criminal stuff, etc. But now all I see is coronavirus, COVID-19, pandemic, and the virus. It makes me sad how much the numbers rise each day.

What makes me more depressed is how pessimistic and hopeless people are for the future. I feel like the people who are trying to save our country from this surreal lifestyle give no reassurance and bring fear.

My middle school might not even reopen until September, and even then I wouldn’t go back. I will immediately start high school. Missing the end of my middle school year is very saddening, because some of my friends are not going to the same high school as me, and I will miss some of my teachers. I don’t like the fact that I will be entering high school as a student who didn’t finish the 8th grade.

I am thankful for those working day and night for all of the citizens in the USA. However some don’t even care. People should take this virus more seriously, and if they don't, the quarantine will last longer. More people could catch it.

At home, when I think about this, I get really depressed and moody, which is unusual because I’m normally a very happy person. I don’t get very excited about many things in life anymore, which makes my mom sad.

I live with my mom, stepdad, and my two half-sisters who are younger than 8. I also worry for their safety, as well as my other family members in Seattle and New York. I usually am happy and positive, but I don’t know anymore. The future is unknown to us all.

Missing Structure
Ritika Khanal, 16, is a sophomore at Mountlake Terrace High School in Mountlake Terrace.

On March 11, my life was completely normal. Up at 4:30 a.m. to read a book, out of the house by 6:15, and on to a structured and intense seven-period high school schedule. The next day, I went back to school for the last time, not knowing when we’d be back.

The first few days after that were rough. Suddenly, I had no impending deadlines, no interactions with my teachers, no structure, and an AP test still looming. For a while, I felt lost. The news I was reading did nothing to help. It was alarming seeing the ways COVID-19 was attacking Washington state.

For the first week or so, I felt trapped within my house. I wanted to be at school, where I would be forced to keep a strict schedule and get to interact with teachers and peers.

When our district started remote learning, I found purpose again. With assignments and deadlines, I’ve been able to create a schedule and follow it every day.

Since my schedule is not as intense as it would be if we were in school, I suddenly have time to pick up musical instruments I haven’t had the chance to play in months, read for longer, and bond with friends virtually, all the while keeping myself from dwelling on all the bad things.

I miss interacting with my teachers and peers in person so much. I hate knowing that so many people are dying because of this virus and that we never know where it will strike next. However, I also think we are in a unique situation where we can be creative, keep busy and pursue hobbies that we once wouldn’t have been able to, all the while staying at home.

Off the ice
Taylor Moe, 16, is a junior at Cleveland High School in Seattle.

I have been playing hockey for 10 years, and I hope to play for a Division 1 university someday. I worry that the coronavirus might affect that dream. The thought of not being able to go on the ice for several months is loathsome. The ice rink has been my second home and the people I see there are my family. I normally visit the rink every day, so the thought of not going at all had never crossed my mind before now.

When I first heard about coronavirus, I wasn’t worried that it would affect me. Instead, I continued going to the rink and practicing for the multi-district camp I had been selected to attend in June. Being the only goalie of my age chosen to attend this highly selective camp, I needed to train as much as possible to impress college scouts.

Later, I received notice that all ice rink facilities and workout facilities would be closed due to the pandemic. I am worried that the multi-district camp, which is my last chance to make it to national camp, might get canceled as well. Making it to national camp has been my goal for three years, because it’s my best chance to be seen by college coaches.

As a goalie, it is hard to do any sort of training off the ice. I am currently trying to cope with my situation by using exercise equipment I have at home and maintaining contact with my fitness coach. In terms of goalie-specific training, I have been using a tennis ball to maintain my tracking abilities. I am doing everything I can off-ice to stay in shape for the multi-district camp and my future in college hockey.

Slowing down, feeling better
Charlotte Engrav, 16, is a junior at Eastside Preparatory Academy in Kirkland.

Anxiety has always had a huge impact on my life, but at the beginning of this school year I felt it get significantly worse. I’d go to school and daily panic attacks would make it almost impossible for me to be in the classroom. I’d walk down the stairs in passing periods and find myself unable to balance on my feet. It felt like there was a permanent numbness hanging over every aspect of my life, and it left me extremely fatigued. I slept every opportunity I had, and even that didn’t feel recuperating enough.

The way I was living wasn’t sustainable. I needed a break.

Then came coronavirus and school closures. As I sat in the assembly and listened to the head of my school announce the switch to online school, I felt myself drop down into that state of anxiety. My legs felt numb as I stood up and slowly walked out of the theater. Everything felt surreal, and I didn’t think I would be able to make it through the month without school.

I’m now one month into attending online classes from home, and I haven’t had a single panic attack. The news is scary, but being home has given me the time to find ways to cope with that fear. I’m trying to incorporate more mindfulness into everything I do, from classwork to walks through the ravine near my house. This slowed-down way of life has been really beneficial for my mental health. I’m hoping that this increased focus on my own mental health will continue long after this pandemic ends.

Calling upon my ancestors
George Olebar, 20, is a senior at SW Interagency Academy in Seattle.

In my native Haida culture, there's an initiation into adulthood. A boy goes on a journey alone and doesn't return to his village until he matures. In a sense, I took that journey when I was 16 and walked away from school. I disappeared for about two years from my teachers, from my family. I wanted to learn on my own. I wanted to figure out for myself who I was, and what’s important to me.

Then, at 19, I returned to high school. I realized I needed my diploma to transition fully to adulthood. I re-enrolled at Interagency Academy, an alternative school in Seattle that lets students finish at their own pace. I knew I needed to enlist people who would help me achieve my goals, like my Uncle Al.

Before the pandemic, Al and I went to the gym four times a week. It gave me stability. I do better when I can exercise. Without the movement, it’s hard to stay in that school chair. If I know I’m going to the gym later, my body doesn’t scream at me to move. I’m not antsy because I’m conserving energy for the gym.

Now, going to the gym has been deemed unessential. My routine has been shattered. I was just getting back on track after the disruption of midwinter break. I’d developed a graduation plan with my adviser, and my workouts with Uncle Al were keeping me focused.

Ultimately, I’ve had to remind myself of lessons I learned during my time on my own: Time doesn’t stop. The sun will rise regardless of how I feel. Procrastination is my enemy. I need to call upon my ancestors to inspire me out of bed each morning and into a healthy daily routine. A routine that will lead me to graduation.

Baking new opportunities
Melissa Takai, 20, is a junior at the University of Washington.

When I decided to make my own sourdough starter, I didn’t know it would come in handy for surviving the coronavirus.

Before the pandemic began, I hadn’t baked anything with yeast since middle school, and those baguettes were better off as baseball bats. That alone was enough for me to cross out “baker” from my list of career choices. Yet here I am, a junior in college studying design, peeking at that list again.

Despite my rocky start, I dug out my bench scraper and tried again. I needed an escape from staring at my cursor all day while my motivation to design slowly faded. As mid-college-crisis thoughts pervaded my head, bread gave me the comfort I needed. To feel the flour slip between my fingers, to see the yeast spring into action, and to taste my buttery creations with family and friends — that was joy for me.

One month later and crumb, autolyse, and biga have become part of my vocabulary now that I’ve had the time to keep my kitchen smelling like rosemary focaccia. Although the stay-at-home order means I won’t have as many taste-testers, I’ve been keeping in touch with friends through Instagram — posting photos of my daily progress, asking what to make, and then doing it.

Although I’ll be continuing classes online this upcoming quarter, I want to make sure I spend less time typing and more time kneading. Although my counter space might not be enough for the two dozen English muffins I’m about to shape right now, I want the challenge.

Once these crazy times are over, I may or may not work at a bakery. This pandemic is giving me time to consider it. No matter what, I hope I master homemade sourdough!

The view from grandma’s couch
Hannah Schoettmer, 18, is a senior at Interlochen Arts Academy in Michigan and is from Bellevue.

When I got the news my boarding school in Michigan would be going online because of the novel coronavirus outbreak, I had four days to leave the campus.

Luckily, my grandmother lives about four hours from campus. She picked me up, and I found myself at her house with a choice to make: I could fly home, but I live near Seattle, and my household has people who are considered vulnerable. They have been on lockdown since before the stay-at-home order.

Not wanting to risk potentially exposing members of my family to the virus, I ultimately decided to remain in Michigan for now and wait until it’s safer to return. Being away from my immediate family is stressful with all the uncertainty surrounding the immediate future—I’m hoping to fly home soon, but I have to take it day by day.

I’m a creative writing major, and our coursework transfers relatively seamlessly to a digital platform — lots of Zoom meetings and Google Doc comment threads are in my future. But I will be missing quite a bit of my in-person senior year, and my best friends are spread across the country. I don’t know when I’ll be able to see them again.

Outside schoolwork, I’ve been trying to stay busy — writing poetry, trying different soup recipes and catching up on reading. I stay connected with friends digitally and hang out with my grandma, which is keeping me from getting too lonely in quarantine. It’s all in an attempt to stave off a sense of impending doom — if I think about the state of things too hard, I start feeling weirdly dissociative. The whole thing seems like the beginning of a movie, except the only thing I can do to save the world is sit on my grandma’s couch and wait.

Teens supporting teens
Gabi Croston, 16, is a junior who lives in Shoreline and attends Lakeside School in Seattle.

Because of COVID-19, I have been practically homebound for about three weeks and am working hard to avoid boredom. I have made a pillow fort, baked banana bread, studied for the SAT, attended a virtual Netflix party, and knitted a hat.

Despite these preventative measures, boredom is creeping in. With this lack of activity comes plenty of time to sit with the other emotions the virus has amplified, such as confusion, anxiety, fear and frustration. After talking with friends and classmates, I can say with certainty that I am not the only teenager feeling these things.

Being a volunteer at Teen Link, Washington's peer-to-peer youth helpline, I heard from teenagers who have diverse experiences. Although staying at home is essential to the public's safety, it is beyond difficult for those who aren't comfortable in their own homes. I can imagine staying with an abusive family member for an extended period of time takes an enormous toll on one's mental health.

A week ago, Teen Link suspended the peer-to-peer aspect of the line in order to comply with the governor’s stay-at-home order. As a volunteer, I am conflicted because I know this decision was made with the volunteers’ physical safety in mind, but it is still frustrating to know there is one less outlet for youth during this time of abundant uncertainty.

I have called into the line before and can say that the resources, support and empathy they provided are needed now more than ever. Crisis Connections has other services taking Teen Link's calls, chats and texts. Still, there is an unparalleled sense of empathy when talking to someone your own age. It is a confusing time for me, this organization, and the world, but I hope we still consider mental health in addition to physical health.

Finding balance
Jadenne Radoc Cabahug, 17, is a senior at Kentridge High School in Renton.

My day-to-day life used to revolve around school. Now it goes like this: help with chores, read books for online school, and paint random things with my siblings. Repeat. I have a severe case of quarantine cabin fever.

This is partially remedied day-by-day through funny coronavirus memes. While older generations are freaking out, buying all the toilet paper in the grocery stores, Gen Z has flocked to social media like Twitter and Tiktok to laugh at the whole situation. Many of us won’t get to experience prom or walking to get our diploma during graduation. I’ve avoided news altogether to prevent myself from panicking like the adults. It’s not that we think everything is a joke, but it helps us cope with the crisis.

Quarantine is not all bad. Before the pandemic, I pushed off many things like painting or starting a new Netflix show because I just had no time between maintaining schoolwork, hanging out with friends, work and planning for my future. Rather than using my free time for hobbies, I studied a little longer before my big test or took a couple minutes to sleep before returning to my schoolwork. Now I have time to pursue mini passions I wouldn’t have been able to pre-pandemic, like painting, baking, and learning Spanish.

This pandemic has also taught me that I need to take risks in life. Not risks like meeting in groups of over ten people for no reason, no. Risks like traveling the world before all the borders close (again) or re-evaluating my self-image. While the pandemic has taken many things from me, it made me realize that I can’t let it prevent me from really living.
